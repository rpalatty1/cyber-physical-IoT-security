Part 1: This type of attack (replay attack) was very effective with this device. Effectiveness depends on several factors - device security mechanisms, quality of signal captured, range, etc. As the device uses fixed code, it sends the same signal for the command; there is also lack of encryption and no timestamps to reject old signals. Gain settings were adjusted during capture to ensure good SNR and minimal/no noise for a clean signal. The relay board and PlutoSDR were also placed close together (in range) to ensure the replayed signal reached the relay board and responded, meaning the attack worked successfully.

Part 2: In replay attacks, the recorded signal is played back as captured, whereas reconstructing the signal involves capture, analysis, decoding data and creating a new signal from scratch to mimic the original. Some advantages are that it's a quick process, simple to perform with minimal knowledge of signals needed, and low risk of errors due to replaying the exact signal captured. Some disadvantages are it’s limited to fixed code systems, less flexible and relies heavily on signal quality, whereas reconstruction can be used on more secure systems, is flexible/customisable and can generate a perfect signal even with a noisy capture.

Part 3: This type of attack is unlikely to work on a wireless car key. The success rate depends on the manufacture date of the car/car key, as old systems (1980s-1990s) use a fixed system, whereas modern systems (from 1990s) use rolling code, and challenge-response authentication (from 2000s) to prevent replay attacks. Modern car key systems are heavily encrypted, using timestamps to reject old signals, challenge-response for two-way communication, complex protocols and overall signal due to modulations such as FSK (compared to OOK). This means replayed signals are invalid, and even if captured it's harder to reuse or modify the signal.
